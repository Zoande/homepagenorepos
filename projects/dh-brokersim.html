DH4410 / Browser Simulation / Playable Vertical Slice

BrokerSIM became a full desk-to-warehouse experience by solving one hard challenge at a time.

The original idea was simple: let players broker high-value deals. The real build challenge was turning that into a believable loop where pricing, trust, shipping risk, client personality, and physical fulfillment all push against each other. This page tells the build story in sequence, including where friction appeared and how each issue was resolved.

The End-To-End Gameplay Loop

BrokerSIM is designed so each step commits you to consequences in the next step. Good outcomes come from balanced decision chains, not one lucky click.

Step 01

Source inventory

Compare supplier offers using trust, inbound risk, condition, and cost before committing capital.

Step 02

Negotiate with clients

Respond to live intent signals while preserving enough margin to survive logistics outcomes.

Step 03

Fulfill in 3D warehouse

Transition into movement-based handling and complete physical dispatch steps in a spatial scene.

Step 04

Resolve day outcomes

Settle delay/dispute impact, profit, trust movement, and challenge completion at the end of cycle.

Challenge 01

Making sourcing decisions feel meaningful, not random

Early versions felt flat because supplier choices looked different but behaved similarly in outcomes. Without tangible risk distinctions, players naturally defaulted to the cheapest offer and ignored the rest.

The fix was to model each offer with a practical set of tradeoff attributes: list price, inbound shipping cost, inbound damage risk, condition score, and supplier trust. This turned sourcing into a risk-managed investment choice rather than a cosmetic menu.

What was hard

Cheap paths dominated too often and erased the intended strategic depth.

How it was solved

Offer values now combine into downstream effects that alter negotiation confidence and final margin.

Challenge 02

Avoiding robotic negotiation responses

Plain random dialogue made buyers feel fake and disconnected from the numbers. Players could not form a reliable strategy because similar input produced inconsistent social outcomes.

The conversation system was shifted to intent detection plus personality-aware response pools. Inputs like discount requests, trust asks, shipping urgency, walk-away language, and threats now map to mood/leverage effects that vary by Friendly, Analytical, and Aggressive profiles.

What changed

Negotiation moved from flavor text to a tactical system with predictable, learnable behavior.

Player impact

Communication style now matters as much as numeric offer values in closing high-quality deals.

Challenge 03

Connecting desk strategy to physical fulfillment

A major risk was disjointed gameplay: if the 3D warehouse felt detached, the project would seem like two separate demos stitched together. The goal was continuity, not mode novelty.

The resolution was to carry one deal-state pipeline across phases: browsing, negotiating, warehousing, and completed. That state continuity keeps player context intact and makes dispatch completion feel earned.

Pipeline win

One deal context survives every phase transition instead of restarting with synthetic UI state.

Design result

The warehouse segment reinforces business decisions instead of interrupting them.

Challenge 04

Keeping outcomes dynamic without making them feel unfair

Deterministic days became repetitive quickly, but heavy randomness made skill feel irrelevant. The design needed pressure and volatility without removing player agency.

Daily events and challenge modifiers were added as transparent external pressures. Demand spikes, shipping slowdowns, and fraud-related risk shifts now alter the tactical landscape while preserving understandable cause-and-effect.

Tuning focus

Events should change strategy, not invalidate careful planning.

Current behavior

Players can adapt through shipping, trust management, and negotiation posture choices.

Challenge 05

Telling the player why a day succeeded or failed

Another issue in early loops was weak post-run clarity. Players could see totals, but not the chain of reasons that produced those totals. Without that feedback, improvement across days was slower.

Outcome summaries now emphasize the core drivers: profit quality, delays, disputes, trust movement, and challenge completion. This tightens the learn loop and gives each new day a strategic purpose.

Feedback design

Post-day summary is treated as coaching, not just accounting.

Why it matters

Clear causality helps players iterate with intent instead of guessing.

Current State And Next Milestones

The project is currently a coherent playable vertical slice. The next stage is depth: persistence, broader event variety, and stronger multi-day consequences so player behavior compounds over time.

Shipping now

  • End-to-end day loop from sourcing through dispatch completion.
  • Intent-sensitive personality negotiation with tactical outcomes.
  • Integrated 3D warehouse completion phase in the same flow.

In progress

  • Balance tuning for risk, trust influence, and challenge pressure.
  • Expanded buyer and event content to increase replay variability.
  • Sharper telemetry for explaining deal failures in detail.

Next major push

  • Persistent profile progression across cash, trust, and streaks.
  • Long-run reputation effects on supplier and client behavior.
