DH4410 / Browser Simulation / Playable Vertical Slice

How a simple "buy low, sell high" idea became a full trading simulation—by wrestling with six core problems.

BrokerSIM started as a straightforward trading game, but making it *feel* real required building a whole interconnected system. Pricing, trust, shipping delays, buyer psychology, warehouse logistics—everything has to interact in ways players can understand and exploit. This is the build story: what broke first, how we fixed it, and why those fixes mattered.

The End-To-End Gameplay Loop

Every choice ripples forward. Pick a cheap supplier with dodgy track record? That gamble hits you during negotiation. Rush a delivery to close faster? Delays cascade into disputes. The game works because you can't "game" each phase in isolation—you have to think three moves ahead.

Step 01

Source inventory

Compare supplier offers using trust, inbound risk, condition, and cost before committing capital.

Step 02

Negotiate with clients

Respond to live intent signals while preserving enough margin to survive logistics outcomes.

Step 03

Fulfill in 3D warehouse

Transition into movement-based handling and complete physical dispatch steps in a spatial scene.

Step 04

Resolve day outcomes

Settle delay/dispute impact, profit, trust movement, and challenge completion at the end of cycle.

Problem 01

Supplier choices were boring and all led to the same place

Early playtests showed one brutal pattern: players just picked the cheapest supplier every time. Why? Because all suppliers produced the same game outcome. Sure, they had different names and prices, but the actual *consequences* were identical. Nobody thinks hard about a choice that doesn't matter.

The fix was real tradeoffs. Each supplier now carries actual risk: a sketchy vendor is cheap but arrives damaged; a premium brand costs more but arrives pristine; mid-tier options offer different failure modes. Suddenly sourcing became an *investment decision* where you're calculating odds, not just picking a number.

The problem

Cost dominated strategy because quality, trust, and shipping differences didn't actually matter yet.

The fix

Each supplier's profile (cost, damage risk, trust, condition) now creates different downstream problems. You're not picking a deal; you're picking which risks to absorb.

Problem 02

Buyers felt like random chatbots, not negotiating partners

Negotiation was supposedly important, but talking to a buyer gave zero feedback. You'd offer the same price twice and get wildly different responses—one accepts, one walks away. Players couldn't learn anything, so they just stopped trying to negotiate and posted fixed prices instead.

Real fix: buyers now have *personalities*. The aggressive broker responds differently to boldness; the analytical type wants math; the friendly one builds trust slowly. When you understand who you're dealing with, your communication style becomes a real lever. Same offer can succeed or fail based on *how* you frame it.

The problem

Dialogue felt random because it was. Negotiation wasn't a system—it was flavor text rolling dice.

The fix

Each buyer type responds predictably to different tactics. Now negotiation is a skill you can develop, not just hope works.

Problem 03

The 3D warehouse felt tacked on, not connected

Early builds switched to the warehouse and players' brains broke. "Wait, why am I moving boxes? Wasn't I trading?" The 2D deal phase and the 3D warehouse were like two different games glued together. Making deals was all strategy; warehouse work was all busywork. No connection meant no consequence.

Solution: one deal flows through the entire chain. You negotiate a specific order, then *you* physically dispatch it. That pallet you're moving is the actual profit you just closed. Mess up the logistics and you lose margin. This turns the warehouse from "mandatory side quest" into "the physical reality of your business decision."

The problem

Modes felt separated. Trading wasn't tied to fulfillment; fulfillment was just UI busywork.

The fix

Same deal state persists across phases. Every successful negotiation *becomes* a fulfillment task. Your desk decisions directly control warehouse work.

Problem 04

Days were either too predictable or felt completely rigged

First version had zero randomness—every day played the same, so optimization became trivial. Add too much chaos and suddenly careful plays don't matter; you just hope dice roll in your favor. The sweet spot is *turbulence you can read*.

Daily events now inject real pressure: shipping delays, demand spikes, fraud alerts. But they're announced upfront and transparent. You know *why* the market shifted; you're not blindsided. Smart players adapt by changing shipping tiers, renegotiating harder, or playing conservative. Skill still wins; luck just changes the playing field.

The problem

Pure randomness felt unfair; pure determinism was boring.

The fix

Events are transparent pressures. They change *how* to win, not whether you can win. Skill adapts to the day's circumstances.

Problem 05

Players couldn't see *why* they won or lost

End-of-day screen just showed numbers: profit, trust, challenges. But the *causality* was buried. Why'd that deal fail? Was it the supplier quality, a shipping delay, or bad negotiation? Without clear cause-and-effect, players couldn't learn patterns. They'd just retry blindly instead of iterating with intent.

Now the day summary breaks down exactly what happened: which deals made money, which ones tanked and why, which trust shifts came from negotiation versus disputes, which challenges you nailed or missed. It's coaching, not just accounting. Players can see the chain of decisions that produced the outcome, so they improve with *direction*.

The problem

High-level stats don't teach. Players saw profit but not the path that created it.

The fix

Every outcome is traced back to specific decisions. Clear feedback loops let players actually learn and adapt their strategy.

Where We Are And What's Next

Right now, BrokerSIM is a tight, self-contained day loop. Everything connects. The next push is building on that foundation: career persistence so your reputation follows you across multiple trading days, richer event variety to keep the market feeling alive, and consequences that snowball. Early wins compound; early losses hurt in ways that matter next week.
